Where Spanish comes from

In two minutes: how a dialect of Latin spoken by soldiers became the second-most-spoken native language on Earth.

It started as street Latin

Spanish is a Romance language — a child of Latin, like French, Italian, Portuguese, and Romanian. But it didn't come from the polished Latin of books. It grew out of Vulgar Latin: the everyday, spoken Latin that Roman soldiers, traders, and settlers brought to the Iberian Peninsula starting around 200 BCE. Over centuries, that spoken Latin drifted, simplified, and reshaped itself into castellano — Castilian — the language we now call Spanish.

Then Arabic left its mark

For nearly 800 years (711–1492), much of the peninsula was al-Ándalus, under Muslim rule. Arabic became the language of science, trade, and government — and it loaned Spanish thousands of words. You use them constantly without noticing:

ojalá (← law šá lláh) almohada · pillow aceite · oil azúcar · sugar álgebra almohada

Tip: a lot of Spanish words starting with al- come from Arabic (the Arabic article “al”).

And the Americas gave words back

After 1492, Spanish met hundreds of indigenous languages. It borrowed words for things Europe had never seen — and then passed them on to English and the world:

papa · potato (Quechua) tomate (Náhuatl) chocolate (Náhuatl) huracán · hurricane (Taíno)
Key idea: Spanish is a layered language — Latin bones, Arabic seasoning, and indigenous American words baked in. Every layer is a piece of history you can hear in everyday speech.
